Breast Cancer

Breast cancer begins when cells in the breast tissue mutate and start to grow uncontrollably. These abnormal cells can form a tumor, which may be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). If left untreated, malignant tumors can invade nearby tissues and spread to other parts of the body.

Risk Factors and Causes

Gender: Women are at a significantly higher risk of developing breast cancer compared to men.

Age: The risk of breast cancer increases with age, with the majority of cases occurring in women over the age of 50.

Family History and Genetics: Individuals with a family history of breast cancer, particularly in close relatives like mother, sister, or daughter, are at higher risk. Mutations in genes like BRCA1 and BRCA2 also increase susceptibility.

Hormonal Factors: Prolonged exposure to estrogen, due to early menstruation, late menopause, or hormone replacement therapy, can increase the risk.

Precautions and Early Detection

Regular Self-Examinations: Perform monthly breast self-exams to become familiar with the normal texture and appearance of your breasts. Report any changes to your healthcare provider.

Clinical Breast Examinations: Schedule regular clinical breast examinations with a healthcare professional, especially as you age.

Mammograms: Follow recommended screening guidelines for mammograms, which are essential for detecting breast cancer in its early stages.

Healthy Lifestyle Choices: Adopt a balanced diet, engage in regular physical activity, limit alcohol consumption, and avoid smoking to reduce overall cancer risks.
